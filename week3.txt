Did anything stand out to you in the video? Anything you could relate with?
What stood out to me was how intimidating programming can feel. There’s this idea that programmers are naturally gifted or math geniuses, but in my experience it’s more about persistence. It’s about sitting with confusion and discomfort, trying different approaches — even when time feels limited — and not quitting when something doesn’t make sense at first.

How can beginners break the stereotype of who a programmer is? Can we see ourselves as back-end programmers today?
The stereotype seems to be that programmers are highly technical, socially isolated, and naturally brilliant. In reality, programming is a skill built through practice like any other.

There are natural predispositions. Not everyone is cut out to be a programmer, just like some people aren’t cut out to be nurses or dozens of other professions. But you won’t know what you’re good at or interested in unless you try. As for diversity, I hope anyone who enjoys problem-solving and building in a computer environment gets the opportunity. The only thing that should matter is whether your mind is drawn to this kind of work.

Are we back-end programmers? That depends on the definition. Can we get a job today? Probably not. Are we learning the tools needed to program on the back end? Yes. You have to start somewhere.

What messages do you tell yourself to build yourself up?
I tell myself I’ve learned this much already, so I can learn the next thing. I can take the next small step toward solving the problem.

What messages tear you down?
Sometimes I tell myself I’m not smart enough or that I’m too slow. But everyone’s path is a little unique.  Some people are good at thinking quickly and on their feet.  Others are better at deep thinking and seeing many angles that others may miss. Comparison really is the thief of joy.

How do we get beyond imposter syndrome?
Imposter syndrome often happens when you learn enough to realize how much you don’t know. The Dunning-Kruger effect explains that early on things seem simple, but as you learn more, the depth becomes clear. Learning a complex subject can take a lifetime. We all have limits, but with perseverance you can find your niche and grow into it.

What do you do when you fail? How do you stay motivated?
Failure is data. Trying something 100 different ways gives you useful information. I stay motivated by looking at how far I’ve come and remembering that I started knowing nothing. Over time, it begins to feel a little more natural.
